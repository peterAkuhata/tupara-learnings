1. Ngā mihi — Greetings & introductions

Greetings are the foundation of te reo Māori. In Māori culture, how you greet someone shows respect and connection.

Common greetings

Kia oraHello (to one person) / Thank you
Kia ora kōruaHello (to two people)
Kia ora koutouHello (to three or more people)
MōrenaGood morning
Tēnā koeFormal greeting (to one person)
Tēnā kōruaFormal greeting (to two people)
Tēnā koutouFormal greeting (to three or more)

Farewells

Ka kite anōSee you again
Haere rāGoodbye (to someone leaving)
E noho rāGoodbye (to someone staying)
Hei konā maiSee you later (casual)

Key rule: The greeting changes depending on how many people you are speaking to — koe (1), kōrua (2), koutou (3+).

Introducing yourself (Mihimihi)

Example mihimihi

Ko Tupara tōku ingoa. — My name is Tupara.

Nō Aotearoa ahau. — I am from New Zealand.

Kei Tāmaki Makaurau ahau e noho ana. — I live in Auckland.

Ko [mountain] tōku maunga. — [Mountain] is my mountain.

Ko [river] tōku awa. — [River] is my river.

Try it

How would you say "Hello" to your class of 25 students?

Kia ora koutou — because you are greeting three or more people.

2. Ngā tūingoa — Pronouns

Māori pronouns are more specific than English — they distinguish between one person, two people, and three or more.

Personal pronouns

Au / AhauI / me
KoeYou (one person)
IaHe / she
MāuaWe two (not including you)
TāuaWe two (including you)
KōruaYou two
RāuaThey two
MātouWe three+ (not including you)
TātouWe all (including you)
KoutouYou three+
RātouThey three+

Māori has dual (two people) AND plural (three+) forms. It also distinguishes inclusive (including the listener) from exclusive (not including the listener).

Possessives — tōku vs tāku

Māori has two types of possession:

A-class vs O-class possession

Tāku (a-class) — things you can control, acquire, or create: food, clothing, work, pets

Tōku (o-class) — things you belong to or that are part of you: parents, body parts, feelings, whakapapa, land

Tāku pene — my pen   |   Tōku māmā — my mum

Try it

Would you say "tāku whānau" or "tōku whānau" (my family)?

Tōku whānau — family is o-class because you belong to your family (it's not something you acquire or control).

Building bigger numbers

Pattern

Tekau mā tahi — 11 (ten and one)

Tekau mā rima — 15

Rua tekau — 20 (two tens)

Rua tekau mā toru — 23

Toru tekau — 30

Kotahi rau — 100

"Mā" means "and" when joining tens and ones. For multiples of ten, the number comes before "tekau".

Try it

If you are a boy and you want to say "my sister", which word do you use — tuahine or tungāne?

Tuahine — this means "sister of a male". Tungāne means "brother of a female".

5. Ngā rerenga kōrero — Sentence structures

Māori sentences follow a different word order from English. The most important pattern to learn is Verb–Subject–Object.

Present tense — Kei te

Pattern: Kei te + verb + subject

Kei te kai au. — I am eating.

Kei te mahi ia. — He/she is working.

Kei te ako mātou. — We are learning.

Past tense — I

Pattern: I + verb + subject

I haere au ki te kura. — I went to school.

I kai rātou. — They (3+) ate.

Future tense — Ka

Pattern: Ka + verb + subject

Ka haere au āpōpō. — I will go tomorrow.

Ka tīmata te hui. — The meeting will begin.

Location — Kei

Pattern: Kei + location + subject

Kei te kura au. — I am at school.

Kei hea koe? — Where are you?

Kei konei ia. — He/she is here.

Notice "Kei te" (before a verb) means "is doing" but "Kei" (before a place) means "is at". Context matters!

Try it

How would you say "She is reading" in te reo Māori?

Kei te pānui ia. — "Kei te" (present tense marker) + "pānui" (read) + "ia" (she).

It means "How are you?". You could respond: "Kei te pai au, ngā mihi" (I'm good, thanks).
