1. Lab safety & hazards

Working safely in the lab protects you and others. You must know the rules and recognise hazard symbols before handling any materials.

Key safety rules

  • Always wear safety goggles when heating substances or using chemicals
  • Tie long hair back and tuck in loose clothing
  • Point test tubes away from people when heating
  • Never taste or touch chemicals unless told to
  • Wash hands after handling any chemicals
  • Keep bags and coats off the benches and away from walkways

Common hazard symbols

Know these symbols

Corrosive — burns skin and eyes (e.g. concentrated acids)

Toxic — can cause serious illness or death

Flammable — catches fire easily

Irritant — causes redness or mild damage to skin/eyes

Oxidising — can cause other materials to burn more fiercely

If a chemical spills on your skin

  1. Run the area under cold water immediately for at least 10 minutes
  2. Tell the teacher straight away
  3. Remove contaminated clothing if safe to do so

Try it

A student is about to heat a test tube of liquid. What 3 safety precautions should they take?

1. Wear safety goggles   2. Point the tube away from people   3. Tie long hair back

2. Lab equipment

Knowing which equipment to use — and how to use it correctly — is essential for accurate experiments.

Measuring volume

  • Measuring cylinder — most common, reads in ml
  • Burette — very accurate, used in titrations
  • Pipette — measures exact volumes

Beakers and conical flasks have markings but are not accurate for measuring.

Measuring mass

  • Top-pan balance or electronic balance — measures in grams
  • A Newton meter measures weight (force), not mass

The Bunsen burner

Two types of flame

Yellow (safety) flame — air hole closed, visible but cooler, used when not heating

Blue (roaring) flame — air hole open, hotter, used for heating

Try it

A student needs to separate sand from water. What equipment do they need?

Filter paper, a funnel, a conical flask (to collect filtrate), and a glass rod (to guide the liquid).

3. Cells & cell structure

Cells are the building blocks of all living things. There are two main types: plant cells and animal cells.

Structures in both plant and animal cells

  • Cell membrane — controls what enters and leaves the cell
  • Nucleus — contains DNA, controls the cell's activities
  • Cytoplasm — jelly-like substance where chemical reactions happen
  • Mitochondria — site of aerobic respiration, releases energy
  • Ribosomes — where proteins are made

Extra structures in plant cells only

  • Cell wall — made of cellulose, gives rigid shape
  • Chloroplasts — contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis
  • Large permanent vacuole — filled with cell sap, keeps cell turgid

Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) have NO membrane-bound nucleus. They are smaller and simpler than eukaryotic cells, with a circular chromosome.

How substances move in and out of cells

Three key processes

Diffusion — particles move from high to low concentration (no energy needed)

Osmosis — water moves through a partially permeable membrane from dilute to concentrated solution

Active transport — substances move against the concentration gradient (requires energy)

Try it

Name 3 structures found in plant cells but NOT animal cells.

Cell wall, chloroplasts, and large permanent vacuole.

4. The periodic table

The periodic table organises all known elements by their atomic number (number of protons).

How it's organised

  • Periods — rows going across (1–7)
  • Groups — columns going down (0–7). Elements in the same group have similar properties because they have the same number of outer electrons
  • Metals are on the left and centre; non-metals on the right

Key groups

Important groups to know

Group 1 — Alkali metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr): soft, reactive metals that react vigorously with water. Reactivity increases going down.

Group 7 — Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I, At): reactive non-metals that exist as diatomic molecules. Reactivity decreases going down.

Group 0 — Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn): very unreactive because they have a full outer shell. They exist as single atoms.

Atomic structure

  • Protons (positive, in nucleus) — atomic number = number of protons
  • Neutrons (no charge, in nucleus)
  • Electrons (negative, in shells around nucleus)

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Try it

Why do elements in the same group have similar chemical properties?

They have the same number of electrons in their outer shell, and it's the outer electrons that determine how an element reacts.

5. Forces & motion

A force is a push or pull that can change an object's speed, direction or shape. Forces are measured in Newtons (N).

Types of force

  • Contact forces — require touching: friction, air resistance, tension, normal reaction
  • Non-contact forces — act at a distance: gravity, magnetism, electrostatic

Newton's Laws

The three laws

1st Law: An object stays at rest or moves at constant velocity unless an unbalanced force acts on it.

2nd Law: Force = mass × acceleration (F = ma)

3rd Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

Speed and velocity

  • Speed = distance ÷ time (scalar — no direction)
  • Velocity = speed + direction (vector)
  • A car going round a roundabout at constant speed has changing velocity because direction changes

Stopping distance

Stopping distance = thinking distance + braking distance

Affected by: speed, road conditions, tyre condition, driver reaction time, and mass of the vehicle.

Try it

A car is travelling at constant speed along a straight road. What can you say about the forces acting on it?

The forces are balanced. The driving force equals the friction/air resistance. The resultant force is zero.

6. Electricity & circuits

Electric current is the flow of charge (electrons) around a circuit. It needs a complete circuit to flow.

Series vs parallel circuits

Key differences

Series: One loop. Current is the same everywhere. Voltage is shared. If one component breaks, everything stops.

Parallel: Multiple branches. Voltage is the same across each branch. Current splits at junctions. If one branch breaks, others still work.

Key quantities

  • Current (I) — measured in Amps (A) with an ammeter (connected in series)
  • Voltage (V) — measured in Volts (V) with a voltmeter (connected in parallel)
  • Resistance (R) — measured in Ohms (Ω)

Resistance = Voltage ÷ Current   (R = V / I)    Power = Voltage × Current   (P = V × I)

Try it

Why are household appliances connected in parallel rather than series?

So each appliance gets the full mains voltage, and if one breaks or is switched off, the others continue to work.

7. Chemical reactions

In a chemical reaction, atoms are rearranged to form new substances. No atoms are created or destroyed — mass is conserved.

Signs a reaction has occurred

  • Colour change
  • Gas produced (bubbles)
  • Temperature change (hotter or colder)
  • Precipitate (solid) forms
  • Light or sound given off

Exothermic vs endothermic

Energy changes

Exothermic — releases energy, surroundings get warmer (combustion, neutralisation, respiration)

Endothermic — absorbs energy, surroundings get cooler (photosynthesis, thermal decomposition)

Rates of reaction

Reactions are faster when there are more frequent and energetic collisions between particles.

Factors: temperature, concentration, surface area, catalysts, pressure (for gases).

A catalyst speeds up a reaction without being used up. It lowers the activation energy. Enzymes are biological catalysts.

Try it

Why does cutting a solid reactant into smaller pieces speed up the reaction?

It increases the surface area, so more particles are exposed, leading to more frequent collisions and a faster rate of reaction.

8. Acids, alkalis & pH

The pH scale runs from 0 to 14. It tells you how acidic or alkaline a substance is.

The pH scale

pH 0–6 = Acid (lower = stronger acid)

pH 7 = Neutral (pure water)

pH 8–14 = Alkali (higher = stronger alkali)

Indicators

  • Litmus — red in acid, blue in alkali
  • Universal indicator — gives a colour for every pH value (red/orange in acid, green at neutral, blue/purple in alkali)
  • Methyl orange — red in acid, yellow in alkali

Key reactions

Neutralisation

Acid + Alkali → Salt + Water

Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen (test: squeaky pop with a lit splint)

Acid + Carbonate → Salt + Water + Carbon dioxide (test: limewater turns cloudy)

Acid + Metal oxide → Salt + Water

Try it

What is produced when hydrochloric acid reacts with sodium hydroxide?

Sodium chloride (salt) + water. This is a neutralisation reaction.

9. States of matter

All matter exists in one of three states: solid, liquid, or gas. The state depends on how the particles are arranged and how much energy they have.

Comparing the three states

Solid: Particles vibrate in fixed positions. Fixed shape. Fixed volume. Cannot be compressed.

Liquid: Particles move past each other. Takes the shape of the container. Fixed volume. Almost incompressible.

Gas: Particles move quickly and randomly. Fills the container. No fixed volume. Can be compressed. Low density.

Changes of state

  • Melting (solid → liquid) — absorbs energy
  • Evaporation/Boiling (liquid → gas) — absorbs energy
  • Condensation (gas → liquid) — releases energy
  • Freezing (liquid → solid) — releases energy

During a change of state of a pure substance, the temperature stays constant even though energy is being supplied — the energy breaks bonds between particles.

Separation techniques

  • Filtration — separates insoluble solid from liquid
  • Evaporation — recovers dissolved solid from solution
  • Distillation — separates solvent from solution
  • Chromatography — separates mixtures of dyes/inks

Try it

Why does evaporation cause a liquid to cool down?

The fastest-moving particles escape from the surface, leaving behind the slower (cooler) particles. This lowers the average energy of the remaining liquid.

10. Reproduction & genetics

Sexual vs asexual reproduction

Two types

Sexual: Two parents, fusion of gametes (sperm + egg = zygote), offspring are genetically different, produces variation.

Asexual: One parent, no gametes, offspring are genetically identical (clones). Used by bacteria and some plants.

DNA, chromosomes and genes

  • DNA is a double helix molecule found in the nucleus
  • Chromosomes are long coiled-up pieces of DNA — humans have 23 pairs (46 total)
  • Genes are sections of DNA that code for a specific protein/characteristic
  • Alleles are different versions of the same gene

Inheritance of sex

Females = XX, Males = XY. The father's sperm determines the sex (X or Y). There is a 50% chance of boy or girl.

Dominant alleles are always expressed when present. Recessive alleles are only expressed when two copies are present (homozygous recessive).

Try it

Why does sexual reproduction produce more variation than asexual reproduction?

Because offspring receive a mix of genes from two parents, creating unique combinations. Asexual reproduction produces clones with no genetic variation.

11. Light & sound

Reflection

  • The angle of incidence = angle of reflection (measured from the normal)
  • A plane mirror produces a virtual image that is the same size as the object

Refraction

Light bends when it passes from one material to another because it changes speed. It bends towards the normal when slowing down (e.g. air → glass).

The electromagnetic spectrum

From longest to shortest wavelength

Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible → Ultraviolet → X-ray → Gamma

All EM waves travel at the same speed in a vacuum (speed of light).

Sound waves

  • Sound is a longitudinal wave — needs a medium (cannot travel in a vacuum)
  • Sound travels fastest in solids, slowest in gases
  • Pitch depends on frequency; volume depends on amplitude

Wave properties

Wave speed = frequency × wavelength    (v = f × λ)

Try it

Why can't sound travel through space?

Sound is a mechanical wave that needs particles to vibrate. Space is a vacuum with no particles.

12. Human body systems

Circulatory system

  • The heart has 4 chambers: two atria (top) and two ventricles (bottom)
  • Right side pumps blood to the lungs; left side pumps blood to the body
  • Arteries carry blood away from the heart; veins carry blood back
  • Blood contains: red blood cells (oxygen), white blood cells (immunity), platelets (clotting), plasma (transport)

Respiratory system

  • Gas exchange happens in the alveoli — O₂ into blood, CO₂ out
  • Alveoli are efficient because: large surface area, thin walls, good blood supply, moist surfaces

Digestive system

  • Enzymes break down large food molecules into small ones for absorption
  • Mouth (amylase → starch), stomach (acid + protease), small intestine (absorbs nutrients), liver (produces bile)

Nervous system

Signal pathway

Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory neuron → Brain/Spinal cord → Motor neuron → Effector → Response

Synapses are tiny gaps between neurons where chemicals carry the signal across.

Try it

Name 3 features of alveoli that make them efficient for gas exchange.

Large surface area, thin walls (short diffusion distance), and good blood supply (maintains concentration gradient).

13. Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it is transferred between stores. This is the law of conservation of energy.

Energy stores

The main stores

Chemical (food, fuel, batteries) • Kinetic (moving objects) • Gravitational potential (objects at height) • Thermal (warm objects) • Elastic potential (stretched/compressed) • Nuclear (atoms)

Energy transfer pathways

Energy moves between stores by: heating, radiation (light, sound), electrical work, and mechanical work (forces).

Energy resources

  • Renewable: solar, wind, tidal, hydroelectric, geothermal, biomass — won't run out
  • Non-renewable: coal, oil, natural gas, nuclear fuel — will run out

Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ (greenhouse gas) and contributes to climate change. They took millions of years to form from ancient organisms.

Try it

Describe the energy transfers when a ball is thrown upwards.

Chemical (muscles) → kinetic (ball moving) → gravitational potential (ball at height). Some energy is transferred to thermal (air resistance).

14. Ecology & ecosystems

Food chains

Food chains show the flow of energy: Producer → Primary consumer → Secondary consumer → Tertiary consumer

Arrows show the direction of energy transfer. The Sun provides the initial energy.

Photosynthesis

The equation

Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen (using light energy, in chloroplasts)

6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Aerobic respiration

The equation

Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water (releases energy, in mitochondria)

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O

The carbon cycle

Carbon moves through ecosystems: photosynthesis removes CO₂; respiration, combustion, and decomposition release CO₂.

Biodiversity threats

Habitat destruction, pollution, overexploitation, climate change, and invasive species all reduce biodiversity.

Try it

How are photosynthesis and respiration opposite processes?

Photosynthesis uses CO₂ and water to make glucose and oxygen. Respiration uses glucose and oxygen to release CO₂ and water. They are the reverse of each other.

15. Earth & space

Structure of the Earth

  • Crust — thin outer layer, made of tectonic plates
  • Mantle — thick layer of semi-molten rock
  • Outer core — liquid iron and nickel
  • Inner core — solid iron and nickel, hottest part

Tectonic plates float on the mantle and can cause earthquakes and volcanoes at plate boundaries.

The rock cycle

Three types of rock

Igneous — formed when magma cools and solidifies (e.g. granite, basalt)

Sedimentary — formed from compressed sediments over time (e.g. limestone, sandstone). Contains fossils.

Metamorphic — formed when existing rock is changed by heat and pressure (e.g. marble, slate)

The greenhouse effect

Greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, water vapour) trap heat in the atmosphere. Human activities — burning fossil fuels, deforestation — are increasing CO₂ levels, causing global warming.

Try it

What type of rock contains fossils, and how does it form?

Sedimentary rock. It forms when layers of sediment (sand, mud, dead organisms) are deposited over millions of years and compressed by pressure.
